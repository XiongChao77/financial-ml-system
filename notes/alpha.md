Where Does Alpha Come From?

Alpha fundamentally comes from the fact that markets are not perfectly efficient — or more precisely, that different market participants differ in speed, cognition, constraints, and structure when processing information.

The common sources of alpha can be grouped into several categories.

1. Behavioral and Cognitive Biases

Humans tend to:

react slowly
overreact
herd
avoid losses
anchor on prior information

These behaviors create phenomena such as:

momentum
mean reversion
post-event drift
mispricing caused by crowded positioning

This is the foundation of many low- and medium-frequency alpha strategies.

2. Structural Constraints

Many market participants are not trading purely to maximize returns.
Instead, they operate under structural constraints.

For example:

index funds must rebalance mechanically
risk parity funds may deleverage automatically
insurance companies and pension funds have allocation constraints
market makers must manage inventory risk
mutual funds face style, liquidity, and exposure restrictions

These constraints generate predictable flows and price pressure.

3. Information Processing Advantage

Whoever can transform information into pricing decisions faster and better may generate alpha.

Examples include:

earnings call transcripts
news flow
alternative data
market microstructure data
nonlinear relationships between fundamental variables

This is also where machine learning is most naturally applied.

4. Risk Premiums Disguised as Alpha

Some “alpha” is not actually mispricing, but compensation for bearing undesirable risks.

Examples include exposure to:

small caps
value
carry
illiquidity
crash risk

In these cases, returns may not come from predictive skill, but from accepting risks that others are unwilling to bear.

Therefore, in practice, an important distinction is:

Are you earning:

true alpha,
or merely
beta, risk premia, or hidden factor exposure?
